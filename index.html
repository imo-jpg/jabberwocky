Jabberwocky is a role-playing game designed by Graham Todd about cooperation and inner conflict, where two players represent competing impulses within a single adventurer tasked with finding and killing a terrifying monster.

Introduction
[ edit ]
V
Tis brillig in the Tulgey Wood, a shadowed and perilous forest wherein danger and opportunity lurk in equal measure. The Beamish Boy, too young to be called a man yet already skilled in wits and words, wanders the Wood with solitary purpose: to slay the fearsome Jabberwock and return triumphant to his eagerly awaiting parents. In his hand is the Vorpal Sword, the enchanted blade which whispers and urges the Boy to bravery and conflict. To succeed they must act as one and balance caution with courage.
Rules
[ edit ]
Setting
[ edit ]
This game takes place in the “Tulgey Wood”, a shadowy and perilous forest filled with strange flora, fearsome monsters, lost adventurers, peaceful clearings, crafty wizards, abandoned treasure, and all manner of other obstacles and boons.
Characters
[ edit ]
One player (taking three tokens) is the “Beamish Boy,” a careful and thoughtful adventurer skilled with wits and words, while the other player (taking the remaining three tokens) is the “Vorpal Sword,” the Boy’s enchanted blade that whispers to him and urges him to bravery and conflict, and the two must work together to try and accomplish their quest (aside from the Sword, the Boy also carries “standard adventuring gear” -- the Game Master has final say on what the Boy might reasonably have brought into the Tulgey Wood).
Conflict
[ edit ]
The Beamish Boy has been dispatched to the Tulgey Wood by his parents in order to slay the fearsome, flame-eyed Jabberwock hidden somewhere within -- a quest that will bring him through a few short scenes in the wood that test his heart and mind, his sword and his words, before the final confrontation with the beast.
Resolution
[ edit ]
The Beamish Boy rolls 1 six-sided die whenever an obstacle in the Tulgey Wood tests cleverness or eloquence (succeeding if they roll on or under the number of tokens they have and failing otherwise) and gives one token to the Vorpal Sword whenever their words or wits get them deeper into trouble, while the Vorpal Sword rolls whenever an obstacle tests strength or courage (succeeding or failing using the same rules) and gives a token to the Beamish Boy whenever bravery or valor make their life more complicated.
Bonus
[ edit ]
Beamish Boy and the Vorpal Sword should try to roughly take turns in suggesting a course of action for their joint avatar, though if they ever find themselves strongly disagreeing on what to do and unable to persuade each other, either player may give the other one of their tokens (if they have one) to end the argument and have their proposal carried out -- and best to do so quickly, as to a spend a while in “uffish thought” (i.e. standing around arguing with yourself) within the Tulgey Wood is to court disaster!
Mid-Game Addenda
[ edit ]
The Game Master should explain all of the rules to the players before beginning the game. The Boy and the Sword share their senses (i.e. the Sword can see and hear anything that the Boy can see or hear) and communicate with each other telepathically. If either the Boy’s player or the Sword’s player has no tokens, then they cannot succeed on any checks that test their attributes. The player that gives the token during a disagreement is the one that “wins” the argument -- effectively they’re bribing the other player with a token to get their way. Only one token can be given this way per disagreement, and by whichever player decides to do so first.
